Finished

Dekelatro

A smaller-scope take on the poker-roguelite formula, built as the final project for my Introduction to C# course. It covered the basic principles of the language alongside more advanced topics — concepts I later expanded on in my Object-Oriented Programming course. The focus was on nailing the feel of a satisfying scoring run — fast escalation, meaningful shop decisions, and the joy of watching a build come together on the final hand.

EngineC# · Custom
GenreCard Game · Roguelite
ContextIntro to C# Final Project
ScopeSolo · Smaller scale
StatusComplete
↗ View on GitHub & Download 📄 Read Full GDD
Design Pillars
01

Scoring Satisfaction

Every hand should feel punchy. Big multiplier moments are the core emotional beat — the game is built around creating and then detonating them.

02

Run Pacing

Blind structure creates a natural pressure curve. Early blinds teach, mid-game challenges the build, final blinds demand execution.

03

Meaningful Shop Decisions

The shop isn't just upgrades — it's a trade-off engine. Every purchase should have a cost, and every skip should feel deliberate.

04

Build Emergence

No run should feel the same. Scoring combos emerge from the interaction of cards, jokers, and hand selection — not from a fixed path.

Key Systems

Hand Evaluation

Standard poker hands with custom scoring weights. Higher hands deal more base chips; multipliers amplify everything. The tension between hand type and card value drives the core decisions.

✖

Chip × Mult System

Scoring is Chips × Multiplier. The two stats scale differently, encouraging players to seek imbalanced builds rather than balanced ones — small mult on huge chips can outperform the inverse.

Shop Economy

Gold is scarce. Rerolls cost money, buying costs more, selling loses value. Every shop visit is a resource allocation puzzle layered on top of the scoring puzzle.

Blind Scaling

Score targets scale faster than the player's natural power growth, creating a steady pressure that forces the build to come online at the right time.

Design Highlights

Score Preview Before Committing

Every play shows a full chips × mult × final score breakdown before the hand is locked in. The player can cancel without spending a hand. This transparency was intentional — players learn the math by reading it every turn, not by memorising a chart.

Gros Michel's Risk Design

The strongest common Joker (+15 Mult) has a 1-in-6 chance to break permanently after each blind win. It forces a real decision: hold it through a risky late-game blind, or sell it first? A single joker creates meaningful tension without any extra systems.

Constellation + Planet Synergy Loop

Planet cards level up a hand type and simultaneously grow Constellation's chip multiplier (+0.1× per use). A deliberate positive feedback loop — players who invest in both systems are rewarded exponentially, which makes the combo feel earned rather than overpowered.

Full system documentation in the GDD →

What I Learned

Building a compressed version of an existing game forces you to understand which systems carry the experience. Dekelatro's depth isn't from the number of jokers — it's from the multiplicative math. That insight shaped how I think about complexity: the best systems create exponential possibility from a small number of clearly defined rules. I also learned how critical feedback timing is; the satisfying "crunch" of a score calculation is as important as the score itself.

Trials of the Dungeon

A cooperative board game for up to 4 players. A group of trapped explorers must navigate a randomly generated dungeon, gear up, level their characters, and grow strong enough to defeat the final boss — The Slime King. Designed and produced as a physical product for the Game Design course.

TypePhysical Board Game
Players1–4
CourseGame Design
RoleLead Designer
StatusComplete · Physical Product
📖 Read the Rulebook

Witch · Healer · Paladin · Warrior — the 4 playable classes

Design Pillars
01

Cooperative Tension

Players share the same win and lose condition. The dungeon is designed to require teamwork — classes cover each other's weaknesses, and the Slime King fight is tuned to punish parties that didn't coordinate.

02

Class Identity

Each of the four classes plays differently by design. The Warrior tanks, the Healer sustains, the Witch deals spell damage, and the Paladin balances offence and defence. Your class should change how you approach every encounter.

03

Emergent Exploration

The dungeon is built from a shuffled tile deck drawn during play. No two runs produce the same layout — encounters, shops, and treasures appear in different orders, keeping each session fresh without requiring pre-designed levels.

04

Meaningful Progression

Gear upgrades, XP, and gold all serve a single purpose: making the party strong enough to survive The Slime King. Every session decision is framed against that goal — pacing the economy was one of the hardest design problems.

Key Systems

Tile-Based Exploration

Players draw and place tiles each turn — Monster, Treasure, Shop, Trap, or Blacksmith. The dungeon grows organically as players explore, with mandatory tile reveals keeping the threat alive.

Combat with Guard & Crit

Monsters have a Guard range (roll required to deal any damage) and a Crit range (roll required for 2× damage). This creates meaningful dice tension even in standard encounters — not just "roll high, win."

Shop & Blacksmith Economy

Gold funds spells from the Shopkeeper and equipment upgrades from the Blacksmith. Equipment degrades with use and must be repaired — creating a resource loop that persists across the whole session.

The Slime King

The final boss acts as the pacing forcing function — everything in the game is designed around whether the party is ready for it. It has unique abilities not present in regular encounters, rewarding players who spec intentionally.

Monsters
Vendors
Design Note

Trials of the Dungeon is the only physical product in this portfolio — and that distinction matters. Designing for a tabletop means no engine to handle collision, no code to enforce rules, and no UI to communicate information. Everything has to work through the components themselves: the cards, the tiles, the tokens. The economy was built around two gold sinks — the Shopkeeper for consumables and the Blacksmith for upgrades — designed to create constant tension between saving and spending. Writing the rulebook was its own design challenge — clear rules that cover every edge case without being overwhelming is harder than it sounds. As lead designer on a group project, I also had to make decisions others would need to trust, communicate changes mid-production, and ensure the final product felt cohesive. It shipped as a complete, playable game.

Post Mortem

Judge Feedback & Reflection

Trials of the Dungeon was presented to a panel of judges. Here's what they flagged, what they praised, and what I'd do differently.

What Worked

  • Combat was praised for its simplicity — roll a dice, resolve the outcome. No overcomplicated systems blocking the fun. Judges noted this was the right call for a board game.
  • Character progression was implemented and designed correctly — players felt meaningful growth across the session without it feeling arbitrary.
  • The objective is clear and well-designed. Players always know what they're working toward: get strong enough to defeat The Slime King. No ambiguity in the win condition.

What Didn't / Would Change

  • Both the tile deck and the monsters should have been seeded. In a fully shuffled deck, players could encounter a powerful monster extremely early with no way to beat it. Seeding both — controlling the order tiles appear and which monsters the player faces at what point — would have created a much more consistent and fair progression curve.
  • The values for both spells and monster HP were too high. Numbers need more careful playtesting and iteration before they feel right. Balance was the weakest part of the design.
  • Board game players want to do as little reading as possible. Spell instructions written in text were a friction point — custom icons to convey spell effects would have dramatically improved the play experience.

Key Takeaway

Physical game design punishes unbalanced numbers more than digital design does — there's no patch, no hotfix, no runtime adjustment. The balance issues and readability problems are things that only surface through playtesting with real people, and we didn't do enough of it before the final presentation. The lesson: playtesting isn't a final step — it's the design process itself. Every session with real players should be changing something.

Brave the Wilderness

A mobile survival game built around a merge-driven progression loop. The player gathers resources, survives, and merges their way up — eventually building a shop that gives their accumulated points a purpose. Getting stronger doesn't require grinding a specific system; it happens naturally from playing well and collecting resources.

EngineUnity
PlatformAndroid
GenreSurvival · Merge
StatusIn Development
Design Pillars
01

Survival as the Engine

Staying alive generates points. The longer you survive and the more actively you play, the more you earn — survival isn't just a condition, it's the core reward loop.

02

Merge as Progression

Resources and items merge upward into better versions. The player grows stronger naturally through collection and merging, without a separate upgrade menu to gate their power.

03

The Shop as a Goal

Merging far enough eventually produces a shop — an in-world endpoint that gives the player's accumulated points a meaningful use. The shop is something you build, not something you're handed.

04

Strength from Play

Running around and collecting resources is enough to become significantly more powerful. Skill and engagement are rewarded directly — not gated behind a separate meta system.

05

Difficulty as a Mechanic

Difficulty scales automatically with survival time — the longer the player stays alive, the harder the game gets. This makes survival time itself a resource: staying in the run longer is always more dangerous, but it's the only way to reach the phases where rare materials drop. The player's real decision is when to stop.

Core Loop
🌿

Gather & Survive

Players collect resources from the world and survive as long as possible. Survival time generates points — the primary currency that feeds into the shop economy.

🔀

Merge

Resources and items merge together to create higher-tier versions. Merging is how the player upgrades — no separate upgrade screen, just a natural consequence of collecting and combining.

⬆️

Upgrade

As players merge and grow stronger, their survivability and resource gain improve. Power scales through engagement — the more you play, the stronger you naturally become.

🏪

Shop

Players spend survival points on stat upgrades — flat bonuses early (Attack, Defense, Max HP) and percentage multipliers later (% Attack, % Move Speed). All purchases are run-only: stats reset each session, keeping every run a fresh economy.

Shop Economy

Points earned from surviving are spent in the shop on stat upgrades. The shop offers two tiers of purchase — flat stats that are affordable early in a run, and percentage multipliers that become meaningful once flat stats are already in place.

Run-only by design: Purchases reset each session. This keeps every run a self-contained economy — the player has to decide how to spend their points with no guarantee of reaching the shop again. It also avoids permanent power creep, meaning the game stays balanced regardless of how many runs a player has completed.
Shop as a crafting gateway: Beyond stat upgrades, the shop also sells rare materials that can't be reliably farmed from enemies alone — including higher-tier crafting ingredients. This means a player who reaches the shop mid-run can unlock recipes they wouldn't otherwise have access to, creating a direct connection between the shop economy and the crafting progression system.
Crafting Progression

Items span five tiers. Each tier gates progress behind a new material source — early tiers use ground pickups, later tiers require actively hunting tougher enemies. Armor stats shift from flat bonuses to percentage multipliers, so a fully geared end-game player feels drastically more powerful than when they started.

Difficulty × Loot

The game progresses through four timed difficulty phases — Easy → Normal → Hard → Expert — driven by how long the player survives. Rather than purely punishing the player as difficulty rises, each phase also unlocks better drop rates and rarer materials. Surviving longer is risky, but it's the only path to the best loot.

Why this matters: In most survival games, difficulty is purely a cost — the longer you play, the harder it gets with no offsetting reward. Here, difficulty phase is both a threat and an opportunity. A player who pushes into Expert phase faces tougher, faster enemies, but is the only one who can access T5 materials. This creates a genuine risk/reward tension rather than just a difficulty wall.
Design Focus

The core design challenge is making the merge chain feel rewarding at every step — not just at the end. Each merge should feel like meaningful progress, not a chore between sessions. The shop as a goal structure also requires careful economy tuning: points need to accumulate at a rate that makes the shop feel earned without being frustrating to reach.

It's worth noting that most of the design decisions documented here go beyond what the course requires. The pillars, the economy thinking, the attention to how each system feels — none of that was asked of us. It reflects how we approach every project: we want our work to feel complete, considered, and worth playing, regardless of whether it's graded.

Key Systems

Every face on every die is individually upgradeable. Damage faces increase base damage; spell faces unlock higher tiers; utility faces improve resource generation.

✨

Spell System

Spells trigger on roll (no mana). Each spell has a full technical spec: trigger condition, targeting, damage formula, duration, stacking rules, and cooldown.

Post Mortem

Why We Shelved It

After around two months of work, the team pitched Dice Bound at a pitch night presentation to secure funding. The pitch was rejected. That was the moment we sat down, had an honest conversation, and decided to shelve the project. Here's what we concluded.

What Worked

  • The dynamic role system — emerging roles from spell weights was the right call. It reduces the need for dozens of pre-designed dice types while massively increasing build diversity.
  • Writing the damage formula early forced clarity on every downstream decision. Numbers with no formula are just vibes.
  • The three-loop structure (core / secondary / meta) gave every feature a clear home and made scope decisions easier — if a feature doesn't serve one of the three loops, it doesn't belong.
  • Designing the spell template before writing any spells prevented inconsistency and made the compendium easy to expand.
  • The offline cap at 8–12 hours was a specific, defensible decision — not a guess.

Why We Stopped

  • The developer wasn't ready for mobile. Connecting to cloud services, handling databases, and navigating the mobile deployment pipeline is a significant technical lift — one that would have consumed most of the project's time just to get started.
  • We were missing critical knowledge for mobile game development: no product manager, no monetisation plan, no high-level economy design. These aren't optional — they're load-bearing for a live service game.
  • Attempting to ship something we weren't equipped to ship would have produced a worse game and a worse learning experience than stopping honestly and redirecting.

What Came Next

The team moved on to a new incremental game on Steam — a different concept better matched to the team's current capabilities. Dice Bound itself isn't dead; it's something I plan to return to and complete solo when the time is right. The GDD is done, the design thinking is solid, and the idea is worth seeing through. The biggest takeaway from this experience: knowing when to stop is a design skill, not a failure.

RunicRPG

A 2.5D side-scrolling RPG designed to showcase deep systems design. RunicRPG focuses on progression depth, character development, and multiple interlocking systems — runes, crafting, equipment, NPCs, classes — that are built to be coherent with each other rather than independent features stapled together.

EngineUnity
ArtTiny Sword Asset Pack
Genre2.5D · Action RPG
ClassesKnight · Lancer · Archer
StatusPre-Production
Design Pillars
01

Systems That Talk to Each Other

Every system in RunicRPG — runes, crafting, scrolls, NPCs — is designed to interact with the others. A rune that boosts strength becomes meaningful when your scroll-enhanced equipment already scales with it. Coherence is the goal, not feature count.

02

Meaningful Character Expression

Three classes with distinct playstyles, a rune skill tree that opens specific paths, and armor that visually changes the player — your character should feel like a build, not a default.

03

Depth Without Bloat

Extensive systems shouldn't mean overwhelming ones. Each layer of progression — runes, crafting, scrolls — should be learnable in isolation before it connects to the whole. Complexity is introduced, not dumped.

04

The World Responds to You

Hirable NPCs that hunt monsters autonomously or join as party members make the world feel like it has agency. The player isn't just moving through the world — they're shaping it.

Intended Systems
🔮

Rune Skill Tree

Players equip runes into a skill tree to unlock distinct playpaths. The tree isn't linear — rune combinations open different routes, shaping the build in ways that compound across the other systems.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑

NPC Companions & Hunters

Hire NPCs to autonomously grind monsters in an area while the player explores elsewhere, or bring them along as active party members. Passive income or active support — the player chooses.

📜

Equipment Scrolls

Scrolls enhance specific stats on individual pieces of equipment — Damage, Strength, HP, and more. A targeted upgrade system that rewards identifying which piece of gear to invest in.

⚒

Crafting System

Every monster drops materials used in crafting. Nothing is junk — the crafting system is designed so that drops from all monsters have potential value, encouraging exploration over farming one zone.

🛡

Armor Sets & Appearance

Custom armor sets change the player's visual appearance, not just their stats. Wearing a full set has both a mechanical payoff and an identity payoff — looking like your build matters.

⚔

3 Classes

Knight, Lancer, and Archer each play distinctly — not just different stat spreads but different combat rhythms and rune affinities. Class choice shapes which parts of the skill tree are most effective.

Design Intent

RunicRPG exists in the portfolio specifically to demonstrate systems design at RPG scale. The challenge isn't building any one of these systems — it's making six of them feel like they belong to the same game. A rune that boosts your Knight's defense should matter when you're choosing which armor scroll to use and which NPC to hire for your next zone. That kind of cross-system coherence is what separates a collection of features from a real RPG. That's what this project is designed to prove.

Tactical Tower Defense

You play as the attacking side — an army of monsters assaulting a fortified tower. Waves grow in size and complexity, and the design challenge is flipping the genre's typical perspective: instead of defending, you're coordinating an escalating assault. The tactical layer comes from composing your wave, not placing defenses.

One-Mechanic Puzzle Game

A puzzle game built around a single, clearly defined mechanic. The entire design challenge is in restraint — finding the one rule interesting enough to carry a full game, then building levels that explore every implication of it. Emergent complexity from a minimal rule set.
